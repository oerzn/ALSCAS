What is ALS?

ALS (Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis), also known as Lou Gehrig’s Disease, is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder that affects nerve cells in the brain and spinal cord. Over time, it leads to muscle weakness, paralysis, and loss of mobility.

Every year, about 5,000 new ALS cases are diagnosed in the U.S. While there is no cure, early diagnosis and treatment can help manage symptoms and improve quality of life.

What is ALS?

Understanding ALS

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS) is a progressive neurodegenerative disease that affects nerve cells in the brain and spinal cord. It leads to the loss of voluntary muscle control, impacting movement, speech, swallowing, and eventually breathing.

ALS is also known as Lou Gehrig's disease, named after the famous baseball player who was diagnosed with the condition. The disease affects motor neurons, which gradually deteriorate and die, leading to muscle weakness and atrophy.

Symptoms of ALS

Symptoms of ALS can vary from person to person but commonly include:

  • Muscle weakness in the arms, legs, or hands
  • Difficulty speaking, swallowing, or breathing
  • Muscle cramps and twitching
  • Loss of coordination and balance
  • Fatigue and difficulty walking

As ALS progresses, individuals may experience increased difficulty with daily activities and require assistive devices.

Diagnosis of ALS

There is no single test to diagnose ALS. Doctors use a combination of clinical examinations and diagnostic tests, such as:

  • Electromyography (EMG) to measure electrical activity in muscles
  • Nerve conduction studies to assess nerve function
  • MRI scans to rule out other neurological disorders
  • Spinal taps and genetic testing in some cases

Early diagnosis is crucial for managing symptoms and planning treatment strategies.

Treatment for ALS

Currently, there is no cure for ALS, but treatment options can help manage symptoms and improve quality of life. These include:

  • Medications like Riluzole and Edaravone to slow disease progression
  • Physical therapy to maintain muscle function
  • Speech therapy to assist with communication
  • Assistive devices such as wheelchairs and communication aids
  • Respiratory support for breathing difficulties

A multidisciplinary approach involving doctors, therapists, and caregivers is essential for comprehensive ALS care.

What is ALS?

ALS is a motor neuron disease that weakens the body’s ability to control voluntary muscle movements. It affects two types of nerve cells:

  • Upper motor neurons (brain)
  • Lower motor neurons (spinal cord)

As these neurons deteriorate, patients experience difficulty walking, speaking, and swallowing.

What Causes ALS?

Genetic Factors

About 10% of ALS cases are inherited (Familial ALS). Gene mutations in C9orf72, SOD1, and TARDBP are linked to ALS.

Environmental Factors

  • Exposure to toxins and heavy metals
  • Smoking and prolonged physical exertion
  • Military service (ALS is twice as common in veterans)

Sporadic ALS (90% of Cases)

Most ALS cases appear randomly with no known cause. A combination of genetic predisposition and environmental triggers may play a role.

Symptoms of ALS

Early Symptoms

  • Muscle weakness (hands, legs, or feet)
  • Twitching (fasciculations)
  • Difficulty gripping objects
  • Slurred speech

Advanced Symptoms

  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)
  • Loss of mobility (requiring a wheelchair)
  • Breathing difficulties (may require ventilation support)
  • Complete paralysis in later stages

How is ALS Diagnosed?

There is no single test to diagnose ALS. Doctors use multiple tests:

  • Electromyography (EMG): Measures nerve signals in muscles.
  • MRI Scans: Helps rule out other neurological conditions.
  • Genetic Testing: Determines inherited ALS mutations.
  • Spinal Tap & Blood Tests: Identifies other potential conditions.

ALS Treatment & Management

Medications

  • Riluzole (Rilutek): Slows ALS progression.
  • Edaravone (Radicava): Reduces oxidative stress.

Physical & Speech Therapy

Therapy can help patients maintain mobility and communication for as long as possible.

Assistive Devices

Wheelchairs, BiPAP machines, and other devices improve quality of life.

Clinical Trials & Research

Ongoing research is exploring new drugs and treatments for ALS.

Living with ALS: Patient Support & Resources

Support groups, caregiver resources, and lifestyle changes can help ALS patients and families cope.
